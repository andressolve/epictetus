The Sculptor's Stone

A Story of Epictetus

In ancient Greece, a young man arrives late to school—muddy, bleeding, and full of excuses. An old philosopher with a limp takes him on a walk.

Part One

Late

Nikos arrived at the school with mud on his cloak and a cut on his hand.

The other students looked up from their benches as he crossed the courtyard. A few whispered. Nikos ignored them. He found a seat in the back and sat down hard, his jaw tight.

Epictetus was mid-sentence when Nikos entered. He paused, looked at the boy, then continued his lecture as if nothing had happened.

But when the lesson ended and the other students filed out to the courtyard, Epictetus remained seated. He gestured for Nikos to stay.

"You're late," the old philosopher said. His voice was matter-of-fact, not angry.

"I know."

"What happened to your hand?"

Nikos looked down at the cut. It had stopped bleeding, but it would leave a mark. "I fell. Running here."

"Running from where?"

Nikos didn't answer.

Part Two

The Walk

Epictetus rose slowly from his bench, leaning on his walking stick. His bad leg—the one that had never healed right since his years as a slave—made even standing up take effort.

"Walk with me," he said.

They left the school and crossed the square toward the craftsmen's quarter. Nikos walked quickly, then had to slow down to match the old man's pace. He felt irritated by this. He felt irritated by everything.

"You were supposed to deliver your recitation this morning," Epictetus said. "To the assembly."

Nikos said nothing.

"You didn't appear. Your teacher waited. The other students waited. Eventually they moved on without you."

"I know."

"Where were you?"

Nikos kicked at a stone in the road. "I overslept."

"You overslept."

"I was up late. Helping a friend with something. I thought I would wake up in time. I didn't."

Epictetus nodded slowly. They walked in silence for a while.

"And after you woke? When you realized you had missed it?"

Nikos's face darkened. "I panicked. I ran. I took a shortcut through the ravine—the steep one with the loose rocks. I slipped, I fell, I cut my hand. By the time I got to the assembly hall, everyone was gone."

"Why did you take that path?"

"It's faster. I thought I could make up time."

"Is it a safe path?"

Nikos hesitated. "No. Everyone knows the rocks are loose there. But I was in a hurry."

"So you chose a dangerous path because you were already late. And the dangerous path made things worse."

Nikos said nothing.

Part Three

The Workshop

They arrived at a sculptor's workshop—an open-air space with a canvas shade stretched over it, surrounded by blocks of stone in various states. Some were rough, fresh from the quarry. Others had been partially carved. One, near the back, was nearly finished: a young athlete, mid-stride, frozen in marble.

The sculptor was an older man named Demetrios. His arms were thick with muscle, his hands white with dust. He was working on a new piece—a block of grayish stone shot through with dark veins.

"Epictetus," the sculptor said, nodding. "Who's the boy?"

"A student. Nikos, this is Demetrios. He's been cutting stone for forty years."

Nikos nodded, distracted. His eyes were on the rough block. It looked wrong somehow. Flawed.

"That's an ugly piece of marble," Nikos said.

Demetrios laughed. "It is. Full of cracks. Hard veins. The quarry master apologized when he delivered it. Said it was the best he had this month."

Part Four

Difficult Stone

"Why work with it at all?" Nikos asked. "Why not wait for better stone?"

"Because someone commissioned a statue. And this is the stone I have." Demetrios ran his hand along a dark vein in the marble. "A better sculptor than me might refuse it. But I've learned something over the years: difficult stone demands your full attention. You can't be careless with it. You can't rush."

He picked up his chisel and positioned it carefully against the block.

"With easy stone, you can get away with sloppy strikes. The marble forgives you. But this?" He tapped the dark vein. "One careless blow and the whole thing cracks. I have to think about every single cut."

Demetrios struck the chisel. A small chip flew off. He examined the result, adjusted his angle, struck again.

Nikos watched. Despite himself, he was interested.

"What happens if you hit it wrong?"

Demetrios gestured toward a corner of the workshop. There, half-hidden under a cloth, was a cracked block of marble. The crack ran right through what would have been the figure's torso.

"That happens. Three weeks of work. Gone."

"What did you do wrong?"

"I was tired. Frustrated. The stone had been fighting me all day—hard veins, unexpected faults. I got angry at it. I thought: I'll just force my way through this section. I swung hard instead of carefully." He shrugged. "The stone doesn't care that I was frustrated. The stone is just stone. My anger was my problem, not its."

Nikos was quiet.

"So now I start over. New block. Same commission. Except now I'm behind schedule and the client is annoyed." Demetrios smiled grimly. "The careless blow cost me more than the careful one would have."

Part Five

Stone and Chisel

They left the workshop and walked back toward the school. The sun was higher now, the streets busier. Epictetus moved slowly, his stick tapping against the paving stones.

"I know what you're doing," Nikos said.

"What am I doing?"

"The stone is supposed to be my life. The difficult circumstances I didn't choose. And the chisel is supposed to be my decisions."

Epictetus smiled slightly. "Is that what you see?"

"It's obvious."

"Then tell me: this morning, when you stayed up late helping your friend—was that the stone, or the chisel?"

Nikos hesitated. "The chisel. I chose to stay up."

"And when you overslept?"

"That was... also the chisel. I could have asked someone to wake me. I didn't."

"And the shortcut through the ravine?"

Nikos's jaw tightened. "The chisel."

"And now you're angry, and late, and your hand is cut, and you've missed your recitation, and people are whispering about you. How much of that came from the stone, and how much from the chisel?"

Nikos didn't answer. He didn't need to.

Part Six

The Stone You Carry

They stopped in the courtyard of the school. A few students sat in clusters, talking quietly. One of them glanced at Nikos, then looked away.

"I want to be clear about something," Epictetus said. "I'm not saying your life has been easy. I know something of your history. Your father died when you were young. Your family lost its money. You've had to work harder than some of these other students just to be here."

Nikos looked at him, surprised. He hadn't thought Epictetus knew any of that.

"That's the stone. You didn't choose it. You can't reach back through time and change it. And I won't insult you by pretending it doesn't matter, or that everyone starts from the same place. They don't."

The old philosopher shifted his weight, leaning more heavily on his stick. His leg was bothering him today. Nikos could tell.

"But Nikos—the stone is carved already. It's behind you. What's in front of you is fresh marble. And every day you make a hundred small cuts. How you spend your evenings. Whether you ask for help. Which paths you take when you're in a hurry."

Part Seven

Bitter Mornings

"How do you bear it?" Nikos asked suddenly.

Epictetus raised an eyebrow. "Bear what?"

"Your leg. The years you spent as a slave. How do you not—" Nikos stopped, unsure if he was being rude.

"How do I not feel bitter?"

"Yes."

Epictetus was quiet for a moment. A breeze moved through the courtyard, rustling the leaves of the olive tree in the corner.

"Some mornings, I do feel bitter. I'm not made of marble myself. I wake up and my leg aches and I think: this isn't fair. I didn't deserve what happened to me."

He looked at Nikos directly.

"But then I ask myself: what is in my power today? Not yesterday. Not the years I can't change. Today. And I find there is always something. Some lesson to teach. Some student to help. Some thought to examine." He tapped his stick against the ground. "That is where my attention belongs. Not on the stone I was given, but on the chisel I hold."

Part Eight

Fresh Marble

Nikos stood in the courtyard for a long time after Epictetus went inside.

He thought about the recitation he had missed. He couldn't undo that. It was carved already.

He thought about the students who had seen him arrive late, muddy, bleeding. He couldn't undo their impressions. But he could decide how he acted tomorrow. And the day after.

He thought about his father, dead these seven years. About the money that had vanished. About the harder road he walked compared to some of the other students here.

That was the stone. Real. Heavy. His.

But the shortcut through the ravine—that had been his choice. Staying up late without a plan to wake—his choice. Running in a panic instead of walking in calmly and apologizing—his choice.

The difficult stone didn't break Demetrios's sculpture. The careless blow did.

Nikos looked down at the cut on his hand. It would heal. It would scar. He would remember.

He went to find his teacher, to apologize, and to ask if he could deliver his recitation tomorrow.

End Note

Epictetus was born around 50 AD in Hierapolis (modern-day Turkey). He spent his early years as a slave in Rome. His leg was permanently injured—some accounts say his master broke it; others say it was a childhood illness.

After gaining his freedom, he studied Stoic philosophy and eventually founded a school in Nicopolis, Greece, where he taught for decades.

His core teaching was simple: Some things are in our power, and some things are not. Our task is to recognize the difference—and to give our full attention to what we can actually control.

"Don't demand that things happen as you wish, but wish that they happen as they do happen, and you will go on well."

— Epictetus, The Enchiridion
